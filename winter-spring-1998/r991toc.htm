We want to invite your contributions for this collaborative publication on "Communications Policy on the Front Lines: Ideas for Change."

Why do Community-Based Organizations (CBOs) need to be engaged in the contest over communications policy? The short answer is that this contest will determine who gets the resources and who does not. There is an old saying that the burden of a compromise is borne by the party who was not at the table.

Community Based Organizations represent a powerful alternative vision for the digital age. Yours is a vision that focuses on serving the real needs of ordinary people, and it is expressed in a thousand practical ways... In church basements and recreational centers and AIDs clinics, CBOs offer a real bridge to the 21st Century for the have-nots and even the have-a-little's. This work deserves support, as much as the work of libraries and schools and rural health care facilities.

Community Based Organizations have a powerful vision and an important practical approach to technology, but your political power is only an enormous potential. This potential exists not because of the amount of money you can devote to lobbying, but because you can reach out and touch and motivate hundreds of thousands of real people. You can show them what communications technology can do to improve their lives. You can make the promise of the digital age real. To awaken this political power, you must begin to articulate a set of policies, and organize support around them. You might be surprised how many friends you have in high places willing to take up your cause.

-- from "Community-Based Organizations and the Contest Over Communications Policy," Mark Lloyd, Executive Director, Civil Rights Forum on Communications Policy (http://www.civilrightsforum.org/cbocontest.htm)

Mark Lloyd's challenge is an opportunity to marshall our forces and move forward together. The upcoming joint issue of The CTC Review and the AFCN newsletter--with contributions from local projects and some of the other major associations of grassroots community technology projects and resource organizations--gives us a triple opportunity:

  • to hear stories about grassroots organizations grappling with policy in their community media work;
  • to present and learn about the specific policy preoccupations and issues of major national community technology organizations in a context that brings us all together and tries to provide some common voice;
  • to see the range of policy support resources we have to work with.
We're planning to publish the hard copy issue in May, to be supplemented with a more extensive online version. We welcome your contributions--both stories from the field and, from appropriate sources, financial contributions to help us distribute our work as widely as possible. Contact us, too, if you'd like to arrange for getting bulk quantities of this special issue. We look forward to hearing from you and spreading the word.
